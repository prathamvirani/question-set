Chapter 6: The Federal Constitution

Foundation of Integration & Vehicle for Ethics and Civilization

The Constitution as a Social Contract

The Federal Constitution is the supreme law of Malaysia, serving as the primary legal document for the country's governance. It is considered a foundational 'social contract,' representing a consensus among diverse communities to protect the interests of all.

Historical Journey to the Federal Constitution

The Federal Constitution is the culmination of a long and complex historical process involving negotiation, compromise, and a shared vision for an independent nation. Understanding these stages is key to appreciating its significance.

1. Pre-Colonial Laws

Long before British influence, sophisticated legal systems were already in place. The *Hukum Kanun Melaka* (Laws of Malacca) and the state constitutions of Johor (1895) and Terengganu (1911) demonstrate a rich history of established governance and law.

2. The Malayan Union (1946)

A post-war British proposal to unify the Malay states under a single administration. It was met with widespread Malay opposition as it significantly reduced the sovereignty of the Malay Rulers and proposed liberal citizenship based on the *jus soli* principle.

3. The Federation of Malaya (1948)

This agreement replaced the Malayan Union, restoring the position and sovereignty of the Malay Rulers and implementing stricter citizenship requirements, laying a more acceptable foundation for the future nation.

4. Ethnic Cooperation & The CLC (1949)

The Communities Liaison Committee (CLC) was a crucial step, bringing together leaders of the Malay, Chinese, and Indian communities to discuss and negotiate key issues like citizenship and special rights, fostering a spirit of inter-ethnic cooperation.

5. The General Election of 1955

The landslide victory of the Alliance Party (a coalition of UMNO, MCA, and MIC) proved that a multi-ethnic political partnership was viable and desired. The first cabinet formed reflected this multi-ethnic composition, setting a precedent for inclusive governance.

6. The Reid Commission (1956)

An independent commission of Commonwealth legal experts was tasked with drafting the constitution for an independent Malaya. They reviewed memoranda from over 131 groups, ensuring that the final document was a product of extensive consultation and compromise.

The Constitution as a Pillar of Integration

The Constitution serves as the main pillar ensuring the nation's strength and unity by providing a clear framework for governance and society.

"Elemen Warisan" (Heritage Elements)

These are the traditional elements preserved in the Constitution that reflect the nation's historical and cultural identity, acting as the bedrock of the social contract.

The Institution of Monarchy

Upholds the position of the Malay Rulers as heads of their respective states and the Yang di-Pertuan Agong as the paramount head of state, symbolizing unity and tradition.

Islam as the Religion of the Federation

Article 3 establishes Islam as the official religion while explicitly guaranteeing the freedom for people of other faiths to practice their religions in peace and harmony.

Special Position of Bumiputera

Article 153 protects the special position of the Malays and the natives of Sabah and Sarawak, while also safeguarding the legitimate interests of all other communities.

Malay as the National Language

Article 152 establishes the Malay language as the national language to foster unity, while protecting the right to use and learn other languages.
