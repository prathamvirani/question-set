Chapter 7: ICT and National Unity

ICT as a Driver for National Unity

This chapter explores how Information and Communication Technology (ICT) influences national unity and the formation of a "plural civilization" in Malaysia. It examines the dual nature of ICT as both a unifying tool and a potential source of division.

The Evolution and Globalization of Technology

The development of ICT in Malaysia is guided by the fifth principle of the Rukun Negara, aiming to create a progressive society. The timeline below highlights key milestones in this journey.

Timeline of ICT Development in Malaysia

  • 1970s: The term "information technology" emerges with the development of the first commercial computer, UNIVAC 1.
  • 1983: Dr. Mohamed Awang Lah pioneers the concept of connecting Malaysia to the global internet.
  • 1984: The first internet connection is established at the Malaysian Institute of Microelectronic Systems (MIMOS).
  • 1989: The Malaysian Computer Network (RangKoM) is set up to connect local universities.
  • 1991: The Joint Advanced Integrated Networking (JARING) project is launched to create a national communication network.
  • 1997: The government ramps up development of ICT infrastructure, including LAN, email, and web capabilities.
  • 2000s: The focus shifts to optimizing internet access, leading to the rise of e-commerce, e-learning, and e-government.

The Relationship Between Humans and Technology

Technology and humanity are complementary forces. Balancing advanced technology with human values is crucial, a concept known as "High Tech vs. High Touch." Without ethical guidance, technology can lose its human touch.

The Importance of ICT

  • Information Access: Provides instant access to information.
  • Global Awareness: Delivers news and events from around the world in real-time.
  • Efficiency: Expedites government and private services like online payments and banking.
  • Business Expansion: Creates vast opportunities for online businesses through social media.

ICT as a Burden

  • Work Demands: Creates pressure for 24/7 availability.
  • Digital Divide: Poses challenges for those not proficient with new technologies.
  • New Social Problems: Leads to issues like cyberbullying, hate speech, online scams, and digital addiction.
  • Terminology: Positive uses are often prefixed with "e-" (e-learning), while negative uses get "cyber-" (cyber-crime).

Ethics and Civilization in the Use of ICT

The core challenge of ICT is not just understanding ethics, but actively practicing them. It is vital to verify information before sharing to prevent the spread of misinformation and slander that can disrupt social harmony.

Guidelines for Ethical ICT Interaction

  • Speech: Use formal, clear, and organized language in digital communications.
  • Sharing: Be mindful of legal implications and take responsibility for the content you share.
  • Advisory Approach: Offer advice wisely and courteously to ensure it is received positively.
  • Debating Etiquette: Base arguments on facts and strive for respectful, consensus-based discussions.
  • Self-Presentation: Maintain a digital presence that reflects moral and religious principles.

ICT as a Driver for National Unity

National unity is the bedrock of Malaysia's strength. ICT can be a powerful tool for fostering this social cohesion by connecting diverse communities and promoting shared values.

ADAM Application

An innovative application from Universiti Malaysia Pahang (UMP) that uses gamification to teach engineering students about ethnic relations, patriotism, and national identity.

MCMC & CMA 1998

The Malaysian Communications and Multimedia Commission (MCMC), established under the Communications and Multimedia Act 1998, regulates the industry to ensure it operates with transparency, fairness, and protects national interests.

Current Issues in ICT

The misuse of ICT poses significant challenges to modern society. These issues require careful management to mitigate their negative impact on ethics and civilization.

Moral Decay

The spread of harmful content, cyberbullying, digital addiction, and hate speech.

Technostress & Technophobia

Anxiety and fear arising from the rapid pace of technological change and the pressure to adapt.

Fraud and Corruption

Financial crimes like online scams, data theft, and hacking.

Invasion of Privacy

Constant surveillance by social media platforms and other digital entities.

New Forms of Warfare

The use of cyberattacks, drones, and information warfare to target a nation's infrastructure and public opinion.

Fake News & Deepfakes

The rapid spread of unverified information and manipulated media designed to deceive and cause chaos.
