The Role of Ethics and Civilisation as a Driving Force

Driving Social Responsibility

Social responsibility, built on fairness and integrity, is vital for a nation's harmony. This chapter explores how ethics and civilisation are the core drivers that cultivate a culture of responsibility, ensuring stability and progress in Malaysia.

Understanding Social Reality: Two Key Definitions

Our social reality is shaped by two distinct frameworks that govern our behaviours and interactions. Understanding both is key to appreciating the full scope of social responsibility.

Authority Definition (Top-Down)

This refers to the formal, structured realities determined by official bodies and institutions.

  • Source: Determined by an authority (government, institutions).
  • Nature: Based on written rules, laws, protocols, and constitutions.
  • Formality: Highly formal, with strict and detailed protocols.
  • Example in Malaysia: The Federal Constitution, the supreme law of the land.

Daily Definition (Bottom-Up)

This refers to the informal, organic realities created by people in their everyday lives and interactions.

  • Source: Determined by individuals, families, and communities.
  • Nature: Not based on written rules; guided by manners, respect, and shared norms.
  • Formality: Flexible and based on daily life experiences.
  • Example in Malaysia: Cultural marriage ceremonies (`adat resam`) or neighbours helping each other.

The Role of Ethics and Civilisation

A society that upholds strong ethics naturally produces citizens with a high sense of social responsibility. A high standard of civilisation fosters national awareness, which in turn encourages volunteerism and responsible actions.

The 5 Characteristics of a Socially Responsible Individual

1. Self-Confidence

Believing in one's own abilities and judgment to act in line with societal expectations.

2. Respect

Appreciating and showing consideration for other individuals, communities, and institutions.

3. Social Communication

Engaging in effective interactions to foster relationships and collaborate towards common goals.

4. Self-Awareness

Being conscious of one's own ethical conduct and maintaining emotional control in social situations.

5. Responsibility

Taking appropriate actions and participating actively in societal and welfare activities.

National awareness is a collective consciousness that drives citizens to act for the greater good. We see this in Malaysia when people unite during national crises, and it can be nurtured through deliberate steps.

Fostering a Culture of Volunteerism

Volunteerism is the practical application of social responsibility. It involves generously providing services without expecting material benefits, for the good of others and the community.

Five Ways to Instill Volunteerism

  1. Produce Volunteerism Leaders: Identify and promote volunteer leaders to serve as icons and role models, inspiring others through their actions and creativity.
  2. Diversify Volunteerism Programs: Create high-impact programs based on specific areas of expertise, both at the domestic and international levels, to attract a wide range of volunteers.
  3. Empower the Culture of Volunteerism: Increase the promotion and publicity of volunteer programs and provide recognition or awards for significant contributions.
  4. Strengthen Strategic Relationships: Enhance collaboration with agencies, NGOs, and international bodies to create a robust network of opportunities for volunteers.
  5. Empower Volunteerism Training: Provide volunteers with proper training and resources to ensure they are well-prepared and effective in their missions.
