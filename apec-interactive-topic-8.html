Authority Definition

The formal, structured realities determined by official bodies and institutions (Top-Down).

Daily Definition

The informal, organic realities created by people in their everyday lives and interactions (Bottom-Up).

Details: Authority Definition
  • Source: Determined by an authority like the government.
  • Nature: Based on written rules, laws, protocols, and constitutions.
  • Formality: Highly formal, with strict, detailed protocols.
  • Example in Malaysia: The Federal Constitution is the supreme law of the land.
Details: Daily Definition
  • Source: Determined by individuals, families, and communities.
  • Nature: Not based on written rules; guided by manners, respect, and shared norms.
  • Formality: Flexible and based on everyday experiences.
  • Example in Malaysia: Cultural marriage ceremonies (`adat resam`) or neighbours helping each other.

Characteristics of a Socially Responsible Individual

A high standard of civilisation fosters key traits that encourage responsible actions.

1. Self-Confidence

Believing in one's abilities and judgment to act in line with societal expectations.

2. Respect

Appreciating and showing consideration for other individuals, communities, and institutions.

3. Social Communication

Engaging in effective interactions to foster relationships and collaborate towards common goals.

4. Self-Awareness

Being conscious of one's own ethical conduct and maintaining emotional control in social situations.

5. Responsibility

Taking appropriate actions and participating actively in societal and welfare activities.

Cultivating National Awareness

National awareness is a collective consciousness that drives citizens to act for the greater good. It can be nurtured through these deliberate steps.

Community success depends on teamwork, commitment, and sacrifice from all members, whether through time, ideas, or financial support.

Building friendly relationships with neighbours is the foundation of a strong community and national unity.

Being observant and aware of the welfare of others, and taking action to help, is the mark of a caring and responsible citizen.

Participating in community activities with a positive and sincere attitude ensures that relationships are genuine and contributions are meaningful.

Fostering a Culture of Volunteerism

Volunteerism is social responsibility in action. Here are five ways to instill this vital culture.

  1. Produce Volunteerism Leaders: Identify and promote volunteer leaders as role models to inspire others.
  2. Diversify Volunteerism Programs: Create high-impact programs based on specific areas of expertise to attract a wide range of volunteers.
  3. Empower the Culture of Volunteerism: Increase promotion and provide recognition for significant contributions.
  4. Strengthen Strategic Relationships: Enhance collaboration with agencies and NGOs to create a robust network of opportunities.
  5. Empower Volunteerism Training: Provide volunteers with proper training and resources to ensure they are effective in their missions.
